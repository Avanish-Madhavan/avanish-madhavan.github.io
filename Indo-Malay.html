Climate Change in the Indo-Malay Region

Understanding the impact of climate change on Indo-Malay region

Species Affected

The Himalayan Quail has not been seen in 150 years, likely due to habitat changes caused by climate and human activities. The Namdapha Flying Squirrel, is at risk from habitat destruction in Arunachal Pradesh. The Leith's Softshell Turtle has suffered a 90% population decline due to exploitation and habitat loss, while the Andaman's White-toothed Shrew and Jenkins's Shrew are vulnerable due to limited habitats and past disasters.

Carbon Dioxide Emissions

Deforestation is the major source of greenhouse gases. In Indonesia and Malaysia, home to the world's largest forestlands, trees are cut down to make way for farms to feed growing populations and for the production of pulp and paper and palm oil, which are big sources of export revenue. Attempts to reduce CO2 Emissions

Between 2000 and 2020, Southeast Asia experienced a substantial increase in total primary energy supply, driven primarily by Indonesia and Vietnam, which have emerged as key contributors to the region's carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions. The energy supply composition revealed a heavy reliance on fossil fuels, particularly coal and natural gas, while traditional biomass maintained significance, especially in rural areas of Myanmar and Cambodia. Although there was a gradual shift toward renewable energy sources during this period, their overall contribution remained minimal compared to fossil fuels. This trend highlights the urgent need for Southeast Asian countries to accelerate their transition to cleaner energy alternatives and enhance regional collaboration to implement sustainable energy solutions, ultimately aiming to reduce CO2 emissions and combat climate change. These sources of energy may not have any immediate impact on the levels of Carbon dioxide, yet this change to renewable sources of energy is pivotal to reduce the global temperature in the long run. Over China, the United States, and South Asia, the majority of emissions came from power plants, industrial facilities, and cars and trucks. For example, per-capita emissions will be higher in economies that rely more on carbon-intensive modes of transportation (like driving and flying), have a higher share of energy-intensive industries (like steel or chemicals) or depend heavily on fossil fuels for power generation.
Migrants from rural areas flock to cities, which emit more heat. New construction in floodplains blocks waterways, leaving cities more vulnerable to floods. And the more cities grow, the greater the damage from increasingly frequent floods and storms. Indonesia has one of the world's longest coastlines at 54,700 kilometres. In the Philippines, which has 36,300 kilometres of coastline, 20 typhoons on average make landfall yearly, with increasing destructiveness. ASEAN leaders aim for a 23 percent share of renewables in the region's energy mix by 2025, up from 10 percent in 2015.

Major Effects

In November, the water level at the Blue Dragon rose to 1.6 meters (5.25 feet) due to typhoon-driven rains. Typhoons and floods in Vietnam and Southeast Asia are becoming more intense and frequent due to climate change. Long coastlines and heavily populated low-lying areas make the region of more than 640 million people one of the world's most vulnerable to weather extremes and rising sea levels associated with global warming.
Vietnam, Myanmar, the Philippines, and Thailand are among the 10 countries most affected by climate change in the past 20 years, according to the Global Climate Risk Index. The World Bank lists Vietnam among five countries likely to be significantly affected by global warming in the future.
The Asian Development Bank (ADB) estimates Southeast Asia could suffer bigger losses than most regions in the world. Unchecked, climate change could shave 11 percent off the region's GDP by the end of the century as it takes a toll on key sectors such as agriculture, tourism, and fishing.
Draining peat swamps releases thousands of tons of carbon dioxide trapped in each hectare of soil. The problem is compounded when farmers burn the dry peat, releasing the gas more quickly. Smoke from such fires has repeatedly choked neighbouring Singapore and Malaysia since 1997; emissions from the most recent incident in 2015 exceeded those of the entire European Union, according to Reuters.
